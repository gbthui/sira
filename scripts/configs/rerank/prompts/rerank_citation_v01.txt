Given a paper (query) and a candidate paper (document), rate how likely there is a citation relationship between them — that is, whether one would cite the other.

Paper: {query}

Candidate: {document}

Papers cite each other for many reasons:
- Using or extending the other's METHOD or ALGORITHM
- Building on the other's THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
- Comparing against the other's RESULTS or BASELINE
- Using the other's DATASET, TOOL, or BENCHMARK
- Providing BACKGROUND or MOTIVATION from the same problem area
- Applying similar techniques to RELATED DOMAINS

Rate the likelihood of a citation relationship from 0 to 100:
- 0: completely unrelated fields, no shared methods, problems, or concepts
- 1-20: same broad field but no specific methodological or conceptual connection
- 21-50: shares some terminology or general approach, but the specific contributions are unrelated
- 51-80: clear methodological or conceptual connection — uses similar techniques, addresses related problems, or shares evaluation methodology
- 81-100: strong citation relationship — one directly builds on, extends, compares against, or uses the other's specific contribution

IMPORTANT: Papers from DIFFERENT subfields can have strong citation relationships if they share methods (e.g., a computer vision paper citing a signal processing paper for the same algorithm). Do not penalize cross-field connections.

IMPORTANT: A paper does NOT need to be on the same topic to be citation-worthy. A foundational methods paper cited by hundreds of applied papers should score high against those applied papers.
